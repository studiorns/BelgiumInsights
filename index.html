Market Analysis and Performance Indicators

Introduction

This guide provides detailed explanations of the key metrics and insights used in our market analysis dashboards. It is designed to help decision-makers understand the technical aspects of our market performance indicators and their business implications.

Each metric is explained in terms of:

  • What it measures
  • How it's calculated
  • How to interpret different values
  • Business implications and actionable insights
  • Practical examples where applicable

The metrics are organized into categories that align with our dashboard structure, making it easy to reference when reviewing market performance.

Brand Health Metrics

Brand health metrics measure consumer perceptions and attitudes toward our destination. These metrics are leading indicators that typically precede changes in actual travel behavior.

Awareness

65.22% (+0.22pp)

Awareness measures the percentage of the target audience who are aware of Abu Dhabi as a Cultural and Sea & Sun travel destination.

How It's Calculated:

Based on survey responses to questions like "Which international holiday destinations offering a combination of both cultural and sun & beach activities are you aware of?" The percentage represents those who respond positively out of the total surveyed population.

Interpretation:
  • 90%+: Excellent awareness, approaching market saturation
  • 70-90%: Strong awareness, but room for improvement
  • 50-70%: Moderate awareness, significant opportunity for growth
  • <50%: Low awareness, major opportunity but requires substantial investment
Business Implications:
  • At 65.22%, Belgium has moderate awareness of Abu Dhabi, indicating significant opportunity for growth.
  • The minimal +0.22pp year-over-year growth suggests awareness has plateaued and may require refreshed marketing approaches.
  • This moderate awareness level positions Belgium in the middle tier of European markets, behind the UK (92.70%) and Germany (85.70%), but ahead of markets like Spain (49.78%).
  • Marketing should focus on broad awareness-building campaigns to increase overall market penetration.
  • The minimal growth in awareness despite significant marketing investment suggests potential issues with campaign effectiveness or message resonance in this market.

Consideration

24.00% (+4.00pp)

Consideration measures the percentage of aware consumers who would consider travelling to Abu Dhabi in the next 3 years.

How It's Calculated:

Based on survey responses to questions like "Which of the below holiday destinations would you like to visit in the next 3 years?" The percentage represents those who respond positively out of those who are aware of Abu Dhabi.

Interpretation:
  • 70%+: Excellent consideration, strong position in consumer mindset
  • 50-70%: Good consideration, competitive position
  • 30-50%: Moderate consideration, significant barriers exist
  • <30%: Low consideration, major perception issues to address
Business Implications:
  • At 24.00%, consideration is in the low range, indicating significant barriers to conversion from awareness to consideration.
  • However, the +4.00pp increase shows a positive trend that should be leveraged.
  • This suggests a significant gap between awareness (65.22%) and consideration (24.00%), indicating that while many Belgian travelers know about Abu Dhabi, far fewer are actively considering it as a destination.
  • Marketing should focus on addressing the specific barriers that prevent aware consumers from considering Abu Dhabi, particularly the perception that there is "not much to do" (+20pp) and concerns about "restrictive laws/customs" (44%).
  • The positive growth in consideration (+4.00pp) despite minimal growth in awareness (+0.22pp) suggests that marketing efforts are successfully improving the perception of Abu Dhabi among those who are already aware of it.

Intent

12.35% (+0.35pp)

Intent measures the percentage of considering consumers who intend to visit Abu Dhabi in the next 12 months.

How It's Calculated:

Based on survey responses to questions like "Which of the below holiday destinations are you likely to visit in the next 12 months?" The percentage represents those who respond positively out of those who are considering Abu Dhabi.

Interpretation:
  • 50%+: Excellent intent, very strong conversion potential
  • 30-50%: Good intent, healthy conversion potential
  • 20-30%: Moderate intent, conversion challenges exist
  • <20%: Low intent, significant conversion barriers
Business Implications:
  • At 12.35%, intent is in the low range, indicating significant barriers to conversion from consideration to intent.
  • The modest +0.35pp increase shows minimal positive movement that requires attention.
  • This suggests that even among those who consider Abu Dhabi, very few have immediate plans to visit.
  • The small growth in intent (+0.35pp) compared to the larger growth in consideration (+4.00pp) indicates a bottleneck in the conversion funnel.
  • Marketing should focus on creating urgency and addressing practical barriers to booking (visa process, flight availability, etc.).
  • The low intent level suggests that tactical, conversion-focused campaigns are needed to convert consideration into actual travel plans.

Intent/Consideration Ratio

0.51 (-0.09)

This ratio measures the efficiency of converting consideration to intent, indicating how effectively we move consumers down the funnel.

How It's Calculated:

Intent percentage divided by Consideration percentage. For example: 12.35% ÷ 24.00% = 0.51

Interpretation:
  • 0.8+: Excellent conversion efficiency, minimal drop-off
  • 0.6-0.8: Good conversion efficiency
  • 0.4-0.6: Moderate conversion efficiency, significant drop-off
  • <0.4: Poor conversion efficiency, major funnel leakage
Business Implications:
  • At 0.51, the conversion from consideration to intent is moderate, indicating that 51% of those who consider Abu Dhabi intend to visit in the next 12 months.
  • The decrease of -0.09 suggests a deterioration in conversion efficiency.
  • This indicates that while consideration has increased significantly (+4.00pp), intent has not kept pace (+0.35pp), resulting in a less efficient funnel.
  • The moderate ratio suggests that once Belgian travelers consider Abu Dhabi, they have a reasonable likelihood of converting to actual intent to visit, but this efficiency is declining.
  • Marketing should focus on understanding and addressing the specific barriers that are preventing consideration from converting to intent.
  • The declining ratio (-0.09) is a concerning sign that requires attention, particularly as it suggests that marketing efforts are successfully building consideration but failing to convert it to intent.

Market Characteristics

Market characteristics describe fundamental travel behaviors and patterns specific to the Belgium market. These metrics help inform tactical marketing decisions and campaign timing.

Average Length of Stay

3.3 days

The average number of nights Belgian travelers spend in Abu Dhabi per visit.

How It's Calculated:

Based on hotel guest data and visitor surveys (DVS), calculated as the sum of all nights stayed divided by the number of visitors.

Business Implications:
  • The 3.3-day average stay indicates a short-to-medium length vacation timeframe, typical of many European markets.
  • This is similar to France (3.2 days) but shorter than the UK (4.2 days) and Germany (4.1 days), suggesting Belgian travelers prefer shorter breaks.
  • Marketing should focus on compact itineraries and experiences that can be enjoyed within a 3-4 day timeframe.
  • Package offerings should be designed around this timeframe to maximize appeal.
  • There's an opportunity to develop weekend-plus packages that include Abu Dhabi as a short break destination.
  • Hotel partners should be encouraged to develop 3-4 day packages with all-inclusive options to appeal to this market.
  • The relatively short stay suggests a need for high-impact experiences that deliver maximum value in a limited time.

Booking Window

70 days

The average time between booking and arrival for Belgian travelers visiting Abu Dhabi.

How It's Calculated:

Based on booking data from hotels, flight booking engines and OTAs, calculated as the average number of days between booking confirmation and arrival date.

Business Implications:
  • The 70-day booking window is relatively long, indicating a market that plans trips well in advance.
  • This is longer than France (61 days) and similar to the Netherlands (70 days), suggesting a more deliberate planning process.
  • Marketing campaigns should be timed to run approximately 2-3 months before target travel periods.
  • This window allows for considered decision-making and comparison shopping by travelers.
  • Early booking incentives are likely to be effective for this market.
  • The long booking window suggests that both digital and traditional media can be effective, as there is sufficient time for consumers to respond to marketing messages.
  • This extended planning period also provides opportunities for upselling and package enhancements over time.
Strategic Example:

For the October-December peak season, major campaign pushes should begin in July-August to align with the 70-day booking window. Early booking discounts offered 85+ days in advance could be particularly effective for this market, encouraging even earlier commitments.

Peak Months (Hotel Guests)

Feb, Oct & Dec

The months with the highest volume of Belgian hotel guests in Abu Dhabi.

How It's Calculated:

Based on hotel occupancy data, identifying the months with the highest number of Belgian guests.

Business Implications:
  • February (2,058 guests), October (1,783 guests), and December (1,980 guests) represent the clear peak season for Belgian travelers.
  • This tri-modal peak aligns with favorable weather conditions and Belgian holiday periods.
  • Marketing should ensure sufficient inventory and capacity during these months.
  • Pricing strategies can be optimized for these high-demand periods.
  • There's an opportunity to develop shoulder season campaigns (January, March, September, November) to extend the peak periods.
  • Summer months (June-August) represent a significant growth opportunity through targeted campaigns highlighting indoor activities and special summer offers.

Advanced Statistical Metrics

These advanced metrics provide deeper insights into market dynamics, helping to quantify relationships between marketing activities and outcomes, forecast accuracy, and market behavior patterns.

Seasonality Index

1.7 (high seasonal variation)

A statistical measure that quantifies the degree of seasonal variation in the Belgium travel market data.

How It's Calculated:

Typically calculated by dividing the highest monthly value by the average monthly value in a year. For example, if October has a travel query index of 5.83 (the highest month) and the average monthly index is around 4.26, the seasonality index would be approximately 1.7 (5.83 ÷ 4.26).

Interpretation:
  • 1.0-1.3: Low seasonal variation (relatively flat demand throughout the year)
  • 1.3-1.6: Moderate seasonal variation (noticeable peaks and troughs)
  • 1.6-2.0: High seasonal variation (significant peaks and troughs)
  • 2.0+: Extreme seasonal variation (very pronounced peaks and low seasons)
Business Implications:
  • The 1.7 value indicates that the market has high seasonal fluctuations, with peak months having about 70% higher travel query activity than the average month.
  • This is significant enough to warrant strong seasonal marketing strategies.
  • Marketing efforts should be concentrated before peak periods (considering the 70-day booking window).
  • There's also opportunity to grow travel interest during off-peak months through targeted campaigns.
  • Resource allocation should follow this seasonal pattern, with increased investment before peak periods.
  • The high seasonality suggests a need for differential pricing and promotion strategies across the year.

Media Elasticity

0.28 (low-moderate impact)

A marketing metric that measures how responsive travel queries are to changes in media impressions for the Belgium market.

How It's Calculated:

Calculated using regression analysis on historical data, comparing the percentage change in travel queries to the percentage change in media impressions over time.

Formula: Media Elasticity = % Change in Travel Queries ÷ % Change in Media Impressions

Interpretation:
  • <0.3: Low impact (minimal response to media investment)
  • 0.3-0.6: Moderate impact (reasonable response to media investment)
  • 0.6-1.0: High impact (strong response to media investment)
  • >1.0: Very high impact (exceptional response to media investment)
Business Implications:
  • The 0.28 elasticity means that for every 10% increase in media impressions, travel queries increase by approximately 2.8%.
  • This indicates that media investments drive travel queries with relatively low efficiency.
  • The relationship is positive but weak (just below 0.3).
  • Media campaigns will generate positive ROI but with lower efficiency than in markets like France (0.35) or Germany (0.42).
  • Doubling media spend will not double travel queries, but will produce modest growth.
  • The low elasticity suggests that the Belgium market is less responsive to media campaigns, and other factors significantly influence travel decisions.
  • This low responsiveness suggests that media investments should be complemented by other marketing approaches, such as addressing specific barriers to visitation and leveraging partnerships with Belgian travel operators.
Strategic Example:

If you're planning to increase media impressions by 20% for a campaign, you can expect approximately a 5.6% increase in travel queries (20% × 0.28 = 5.6%). This helps set realistic expectations for campaign performance and ROI calculations.

YoY Growth Consistency

0.78 (high consistency)

A statistical measure that evaluates how uniform or predictable the year-over-year growth patterns are for the Belgium travel market.

How It's Calculated:

Typically calculated using a statistical measure like the coefficient of variation (inverse) or correlation coefficient of YoY growth rates across different time periods. The scale ranges from 0 to 1, where 0 represents completely inconsistent growth (highly volatile) and 1 represents perfectly consistent growth (same growth rate across all periods).

Interpretation:
  • <0.5: Low consistency (high volatility, unpredictable patterns)
  • 0.5-0.7: Moderate consistency (some predictability with variations)
  • 0.7-0.9: High consistency (reliable patterns with minor variations)
  • >0.9: Very high consistency (highly predictable patterns)
Business Implications:
  • The 0.78 consistency indicates that there is high predictability in how the market behaves year-over-year.
  • Forecasting can be done with good confidence.
  • Most months will show similar growth patterns to previous years.
  • Only occasional months may show unexpected variations requiring adaptive strategies.
  • Marketing teams can plan with greater certainty about expected outcomes.
  • Historical patterns provide reliable guidance for future planning.
  • This high consistency (0.78) contributes to a relatively narrow forecast confidence interval of ±8.2%.

Forecast Confidence Interval

±8.2%

A statistical measure indicating the range within which we expect the actual 2025 travel query values to fall with a certain level of confidence (typically 95%).

How It's Calculated:

Derived from statistical analysis of historical forecast accuracy, market volatility, and model parameters. It represents the standard error of the forecast multiplied by a factor corresponding to the desired confidence level (typically 1.96 for 95% confidence).

Interpretation:
  • <5%: Very high forecast precision, highly reliable predictions
  • 5-10%: Good forecast precision, reliable predictions with moderate variance
  • 10-15%: Moderate forecast precision, usable predictions with significant variance
  • >15%: Low forecast precision, high uncertainty in predictions
Business Implications:
  • The ±8.2% confidence interval means that if we forecast a travel query index of 4.56 for a particular month, the actual value is expected to fall between 4.19 and 4.93 with 95% confidence.
  • This relatively narrow interval indicates a reliable forecast for the Belgium market.
  • Marketing teams should plan with appropriate contingencies, knowing the potential range of outcomes.
  • Budget allocations should include flexibility to adjust based on actual performance within this range.
  • The confidence interval suggests that forecasts can be used with good confidence for planning purposes.

Barriers to Visitation

Understanding the reasons why Belgian travelers might not consider visiting Abu Dhabi is crucial for developing targeted marketing strategies that address these concerns and misconceptions.

Top Barriers to Visitation

Q4'24 vs Q4'23

The primary reasons cited by Belgian respondents for not considering Abu Dhabi as a travel destination, based on survey data.

How It's Calculated:

Based on survey responses to the question "Why would you not consider visiting Abu Dhabi now or in the future?" among those who indicated they would not consider Abu Dhabi as a destination. Respondents could select multiple reasons.

Top 5 Barriers (Q4'24):
  • Restrictive laws and customs: 44% (+1pp year-over-year)
  • Not value for money: 34% (+11pp year-over-year)
  • Not much to do there: 33% (+20pp year-over-year)
  • Other places I would rather visit: 30% (-12pp year-over-year)
  • Too expensive: 30% (-13pp year-over-year)
Significant Changes (Year-over-Year):
  • Largest Increases:
    • "Not much to do there": +20pp (13% → 33%)
    • "Already visited before": +16pp (13% → 29%)
    • "No direct flights": +13pp (0% → 13%)
  • Largest Decreases:
    • "Too expensive": -13pp (43% → 30%)
    • "Other places I would rather visit": -12pp (43% → 30%)
Business Implications:
  • The perception of "restrictive laws and customs" remains the top barrier (44%), indicating a need for educational marketing that clarifies misconceptions about local regulations and customs.
  • The significant increase in the perception that there is "not much to do" (+20pp) is particularly concerning and suggests a failure to effectively communicate Abu Dhabi's diverse offerings to the Belgian market.
  • Value perception has deteriorated, with "not value for money" increasing by 11pp to 34%, despite the perception of being "too expensive" decreasing by 13pp. This suggests that while price sensitivity may be decreasing, Belgians are questioning what they get for their money.
  • The emergence of "no direct flights" as a barrier (from 0% to 13%) indicates a practical obstacle that should be addressed through airline partnerships or better communication about convenient connecting options.
  • The increase in "already visited before" (+16pp) could be interpreted positively as indicating growth in visitation, but also suggests a need for marketing that highlights new attractions and experiences to encourage repeat visits.
  • The decrease in "other places I would rather visit" (-12pp) is positive, suggesting Abu Dhabi is becoming more competitive in the consideration set.
  • The perception of being "not authentic enough" remains high at 29%, indicating a need for marketing that showcases authentic cultural experiences and local heritage.
Strategic Recommendation:

Develop a multi-faceted campaign specifically for the Belgian market that addresses the top barriers: (1) Create educational content that clarifies misconceptions about local laws and customs, emphasizing the welcoming nature of Abu Dhabi for international visitors; (2) Showcase the diversity of activities available, particularly highlighting cultural attractions that align with Belgian interests; (3) Emphasize value-for-money aspects by creating content that demonstrates the quality and uniqueness of experiences relative to cost; (4) Partner with airlines to improve flight connectivity or create package deals that make the journey more convenient and affordable; and (5) Highlight authentic cultural experiences that counter the perception of artificiality.

Perception vs Reality

Analysis of the gap between Belgian travelers' perceptions of Abu Dhabi and the reality, with recommendations for addressing misconceptions.

Key Perception Gaps:

Business Implications:
  • The perception gaps identified represent significant opportunities for targeted marketing and product development to address specific Belgian market concerns.
  • The most urgent issue to address is the growing perception that there is "not much to do" in Abu Dhabi, which directly impacts consideration and intent metrics.
  • The value proposition needs to be better articulated, focusing on the quality and uniqueness of experiences rather than just price points.
  • The authenticity concern suggests a need for marketing that goes beyond glossy images of modern attractions to showcase genuine cultural experiences and local interactions.
  • The flight connectivity issue may require both improved actual connections and better communication about existing options.
  • The decrease in price sensitivity ("too expensive" -13pp) represents an opportunity to focus marketing on premium experiences that justify the cost.
  • The increase in "already visited" (+16pp) suggests a need for marketing that highlights new attractions and experiences to encourage repeat visits.

Conclusion & Strategic Recommendations

Based on the comprehensive analysis of the Belgium market, we have identified key insights and strategic recommendations to enhance Abu Dhabi's position and performance in this market.

Market Summary

The Belgium market presents a moderate-potential opportunity for Abu Dhabi, characterized by:

  • Moderate awareness (65.22%) with minimal growth (+0.22pp)
  • Low but growing consideration (24.00%, +4.00pp)
  • Low intent (12.35%, +0.35pp) with declining conversion efficiency (0.51, -0.09)
  • Short average stays (3.3 days) with a relatively long booking window (70 days)
  • Strong interest in cultural attractions (42% of searches) and luxury accommodations (48% of searches)
  • Significant barriers around perceptions of restrictive laws (44%), value for money (34%), and lack of activities (33%)

Belgian travelers show a distinct preference for cultural experiences combined with luxury accommodations, particularly in waterfront or central locations. They plan well in advance but stay for relatively short periods, suggesting a preference for high-impact, concentrated experiences.

Key Opportunities

  • Cultural-Luxury Positioning: Leverage the strong Belgian interest in cultural attractions (42%) and luxury accommodations (48%) to position Abu Dhabi as a premium cultural destination.
  • Consideration Growth: Build on the positive momentum in consideration (+4.00pp) by addressing the specific barriers that prevent aware consumers from considering Abu Dhabi.
  • Shoulder Season Development: Expand beyond the current peak months (Feb, Oct, Dec) by developing targeted campaigns for shoulder seasons.
  • Weekend-Plus Packages: Create specialized 3-4 day packages that maximize experiences within the typical Belgian length of stay.
  • Educational Marketing: Address misconceptions about local laws and customs, which remain the top barrier to visitation (44%).

Strategic Recommendations

  1. Develop a "Cultural Immersion" Campaign: Create marketing that highlights Abu Dhabi's cultural richness, focusing on the Sheikh Zayed Grand Mosque, Qasr Al Watan, and Louvre Abu Dhabi, which align with Belgian interests. Include authentic local experiences to counter the perception of inauthenticity.
  2. Launch a "More Than You Think" Content Series: Address the growing perception that there is "not much to do" (+20pp) by showcasing the diversity of experiences available in Abu Dhabi, particularly focusing on cultural and entertainment options.
  3. Create Luxury-Cultural Packages: Partner with luxury waterfront properties to develop packages that combine cultural experiences with premium accommodations, targeting the Belgian preference for both.
  4. Implement a "Know Before You Go" Educational Campaign: Develop content that clarifies misconceptions about local laws and customs, emphasizing the welcoming nature of Abu Dhabi for international visitors.
  5. Enhance Air Connectivity: Work with airlines to improve flight options from Belgium or develop partnerships that make connecting flights more seamless and attractive.
  6. Optimize Campaign Timing: Align marketing campaigns with the 70-day booking window, ensuring major pushes occur 2-3 months before target travel periods.
  7. Develop Value Messaging: Create content that emphasizes the quality and uniqueness of experiences relative to cost, addressing the growing concern about value for money (+11pp).

Implementation Timeline

  • Immediate (Q2 2025): Launch the "More Than You Think" content series and "Know Before You Go" educational campaign to address the most pressing perception issues.
  • Short-term (Q3 2025): Develop and promote luxury-cultural packages in partnership with key accommodation providers, targeting the October peak season.
  • Medium-term (Q4 2025): Implement airline partnerships to enhance connectivity and develop value-focused messaging for the winter season.
  • Long-term (2026): Develop comprehensive shoulder season campaigns and expand the cultural immersion offerings to include new attractions and experiences.

By implementing these strategic recommendations, Abu Dhabi can address the specific needs and concerns of the Belgian market, leveraging its strengths in cultural offerings and luxury accommodations while mitigating the barriers to visitation. This targeted approach should help improve conversion through the funnel, particularly addressing the gap between growing consideration and stagnant intent.
